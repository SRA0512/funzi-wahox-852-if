Research Question & Purpose

Understanding what you're asking and why it matters.

1.1 Core Research Question
The Exact Question You're Answering

Can a computational scaffold-design engine — informed by irregular wound-boundary geometry and simulated tissue-growth strain — generate scaffold architectures that outperform standard periodic lattice designs in preserving pore structure and reducing local deformation?

📌 Key Word: Outperform
Your engine doesn't just make scaffolds — it makes scaffolds that score BETTER on quantifiable metrics. This is the measurable claim that judges will probe.
1.2 Why This Matters
The Clinical Problem

Skin grafts and tissue scaffolds must support healing tissue whose geometry is irregular and whose mechanical environment changes continuously as surrounding tissue grows, contracts, and remodels.

Standard scaffolds use simple repeating (periodic) geometries — square grids, regular honeycombs — easy to model but they handle complex strain poorly. When stretched, identical cells deform identically, causing pore collapse in high-strain zones and wasted material in low-strain zones.

⚠️ The Problem With Periodic Scaffolds
In a periodic design, one weak point = every weak point. If one cell fails under strain, every identical cell fails at the same load. Aperiodic and auxetic designs break this symmetry.
Why is this project in Math & Computational Sciences rather than Biomedical Engineering?
The primary contribution IS the algorithm — boundary extraction, geometric feature computation, multi-metric scoring, Hat monotile tiling, auxetic lattice generation, weight-sensitivity analysis, and hybrid scaffold construction. Physical testing validates the model; it is not the model itself. The core question is whether a mathematical scoring function can predict and improve scaffold performance better than manual design selection. The biology is the motivation; the math is the contribution.
You say your project "matters" — but you didn't test on real wounds or cells. How is this clinically relevant?
The clinical relevance is in the geometry and algorithm, not the material. The mathematical principles governing pore retention under strain are material-independent. If the computational engine correctly predicts which geometric architecture handles strain best, that prediction holds whether the material is TPU, PCL, or collagen. The TPU is a validation substrate, not a clinical claim. Full cell culture and clinical validation are explicitly listed as future work in Section 13.3.

Module 3

The Hat Monotile

The mathematical novelty at the heart of your project.

Primary Novelty Claim
What Is the Hat Monotile?

The Hat monotile (Smith, Myers, Kaplan, Goodman-Strauss; Combinatorial Theory, June 2024) is the world's first true aperiodic monotile — a single 13-sided polygon that tiles the entire plane using geometry alone, with no additional matching rules required.

🔑 What Aperiodic Means
Aperiodic = the pattern NEVER repeats. There is no unit cell you could copy-paste to recreate it. No matter how far you extend the tiling, you will never find the same local arrangement twice. This is mathematically proven.

This gives it structural properties no periodic design can have: no translational symmetry means no repeating weak points, and strain concentrations cannot propagate predictably through the structure.

Implementation
Recursive Metatile Substitution

Your implementation uses the mathematically correct approach from the primary paper: H/T/P/F metatile substitution. Starting with a small set of initial tiles, each metatile is recursively subdivided into smaller tiles, generating the aperiodic pattern at any scale.

SCALE = 2.6 # mm per unit
STRUT_WIDTH = 0.9 # mm
NODE_RATIO = 1.5 # merged node diameter multiplier
SUBSTITUTION_LEVELS = 4

# Result: Group5_Hat.dxf
# Connectivity: 100%
# Mean pore diameter: 3.91mm ✅ (target 3-5mm)
⚙️ Node Merging — Critical Engineering Step
At tile junctions where 3+ edges meet at angles <45°, razor-thin geometry forms that will fail during FDM printing. Your code identifies these junctions and replaces them with merged circular nodes (diameter = 1.5× strut width). This preserves the aperiodic geometry while making it physically printable.
Scientific Support
Why Aperiodic = Better for Scaffolds

Rosa et al. (Advanced Materials, 2025) showed that quasicrystalline lattice architectures outperform periodic designs in distributing strain without global buckling instabilities. The Hat tiling is quasiperiodic with hexagonal (C6) symmetry (Socolar, 2023).

Module 6 confirmed this: Hat ranked #2 of 5 scaffolds, outperforming all periodic auxetic designs on boundary fit and pore uniformity — despite being the newest, most mathematically complex geometry.

Is this the first time the Hat monotile has been used in scaffold design?
To the best of my knowledge and literature review, yes. The Hat monotile was only formally published in June 2024. This project applies it to scaffold architecture for the first time at any research level. Rosa et al. (2025) used Penrose tiling (a different aperiodic structure) for lattice architectures, but no published work has applied the Hat specifically to biomedical scaffolds. This is a genuine novelty claim, not an incremental one.
The Hat tile requires both a tile and its mirror image. Doesn't that violate the "single tile" claim?
This is a great question that shows deep knowledge of the literature. The original 2023 paper required both the Hat and its mirror (the "anti-Hat"). The Spectre tile, published in September 2024, is the true chiral aperiodic monotile that tiles without reflections. My implementation correctly includes both the Hat and anti-Hat orientations, which is mathematically required for a valid Hat tiling. The Spectre is listed as future work in Section 13.3.

Module 4

Auxetic Lattice Designs

The physics of negative Poisson's ratio and why it matters for scaffolds.

Core Concept
What Is a Negative Poisson's Ratio?

Poisson's ratio (ν) describes how a material changes width when you stretch it lengthwise. Most materials narrow when stretched (ν > 0). Auxetic materials expand laterally when stretched (ν < 0).

🧠 Why This Is Perfect for Scaffolds
When healing tissue stretches a scaffold, a normal material's pores would narrow and collapse. An auxetic scaffold's pores would actually OPEN, maintaining space for cell infiltration. This is why negative Poisson's ratio is the most heavily weighted metric (30%) in your scoring engine.

Poisson's ratio formula for re-entrant honeycomb (Lvov et al. 2020):

ν = −sin²(θ) / (α − cosθ)·cosθ
α = h/l = 2.0 · · · h = 8mm · · · l = 4mm · · · t = 1mm

θ = 55° was chosen as the article-backed optimal angle. θ = 70° is the upper tested range from Lvov et al. The Rotating Squares design uses a different mechanism — squares connected at corners rotate outward under tension rather than relying on rib angles.

Lvov et al. tested compression. Your assay uses tension. Is the Poisson's ratio still valid?
For small strains, Poisson's ratio is symmetric — the material responds the same way in tension as compression in the linear elastic regime. Lvov et al.'s theoretical formula (ν = −sin²θ / (α−cosθ)cosθ) is derived from the geometry of the unit cell, not from the loading direction. My assay applies 10-20% strain, which is within the small-strain regime for TPU. The cyclic compression data from Lvov et al. also shows no layer delamination after 500 cycles, which validates TPU as a suitable test material for the geometry comparison.

Module 5

The Strain Map

Physics-based simulation of wound stress distribution.

The Core Math
Airy Stress Function & Biharmonic Equation

George Biddell Airy (1862) discovered that for a thin elastic plate, all three stress components can be derived from a single function φ (phi):

σ_xx = d²φ/dy²    σ_yy = d²φ/dx²    τ_xy = −d²φ/dxdy
φ must satisfy: ∇⁴φ = 0 (biharmonic equation)

∇⁴φ = 0 expanded: d⁴φ/dx⁴ + 2·d⁴φ/dx²dy² + d⁴φ/dy⁴ = 0

💡 Why One Function?
Instead of solving for 3 unknowns (σ_xx, σ_yy, τ_xy) at every point simultaneously, you solve for 1 function φ everywhere, then differentiate. This is Airy's insight from 1862 that your code uses 164 years later.

Three Loading Scenarios + Von Mises
Computing the Final Strain Value

Scenario A — Horizontal Tension: b[row] = (x − cx) / (maxx − minx)

Stress increases linearly left-to-right. Simulates skin stretching over a bending joint. Validated: left/right mean strain (0.095) > top/bottom (0.050) ✅

Scenario B — Biaxial Tension: b[row] = ((x−cx)² + (y−cy)²) / (diagonal²)

Stress proportional to squared distance from center. Radially symmetric. Note: r=0.990 correlation with Scenario C — documented as limitation.

Scenario C — Radial Outward: b[row] = r / r_max

Stress proportional to linear distance from center. Most biologically realistic. Validated: boundary strain (0.238) >> interior (0.055) ✅

After solving, stress components are combined using von Mises criterion (1913):

σ_vm = √(σ_xx² − σ_xx·σ_yy + σ_yy² + 3·τ_xy²)
Normalized to 0–1 range across all interior points

Why are Scenarios B and C 99% correlated? Is this a problem?
Both B and C are radially symmetric loading patterns — B uses squared distance from center, C uses linear distance. For a roughly circular wound, both produce the same spatial pattern: highest stress at the boundary, lowest at the center. This is documented as a known limitation in Section 13.2. It slightly over-weights radial loading in the mean strain calculation used by Module 6. Scenario A (r=0.807 with B, 0.830 with C) is meaningfully distinct. Future work: replace B with diagonal tension (b[row] = (x−cx+y−cy)/diagonal) to improve independence.

Module 7

Hybrid Scaffold — Group 6

The algorithm's actual design output — your central engineering contribution.

What It Does
Region-Adaptive Scaffold Design

The hybrid scaffold divides the wound into 5 zones using k-means clustering, then assigns the highest-scoring motif to each zone based on local strain and geometry.

k-means clustering (k=5) → 5 wound zones
For each zone: score all 5 motifs using zone-adapted weights
High-strain zone: SC weight → 0.35
Boundary zone: BF weight → 0.30
Assign best motif per zone
Connect zones with 1.2mm perimeter ring struts
Export: hybrid_scaffold.dxf + hybrid_zones.png
🔑 The Zone-Ring Method
Zone boundaries are connected by structural perimeter rings (not interpolated blends). The ring sits on top of both zone scaffolds, spanning the boundary, and acts as the connector. This is explicitly designed to be fully printable in TPU and geometrically validated in Shapely before any printing.
Why k-means with k=5? Why not k=3 or k=7?
k=5 was chosen to match the number of available scaffold motifs (Groups 1-5), so every motif has an opportunity to be assigned to at least one zone. With k=3, some motifs would never be selected. With k=7, zones would be smaller than individual scaffold cells, making the motif assignment geometrically meaningless. Future work could include an elbow method or silhouette analysis to optimize k for each wound shape — this is a valid methodological improvement to propose to judges.
How do you know the hybrid scaffold will actually outperform the individual benchmark scaffolds?
Computationally, the hybrid MUST score at least as well as the best single motif, because each zone is assigned the highest-scoring motif for that zone's specific conditions. In the worst case, all zones get the same motif, giving the same score as that motif alone. In the expected case, different zones have different strain and geometry profiles, so different motifs win in different zones, improving the aggregate score. Physical validation tests whether this computational advantage translates to measurable differences in pore retention and bead retention — that's what the assays are for.

Statistics & Validation

How you prove the engine actually works.

Primary Validation
Pore-Level Spearman Correlation

The most important statistic in your project is ρ_pore — the Spearman rank correlation between the engine's predicted local porosity and the physically measured pore retention at every individual pore across all 18 scaffolds.

ρ_pore = spearmanr(predicted[], measured[])   n > 200
Target: significant positive ρ at p < 0.001
✅ Why This Is Powerful
Most scoring engine papers validate at the scaffold level (n=5 or 6 data points). You validate at the PORE level (n>200). This means the engine predicts performance not just on average, but at the resolution of individual architectural elements. No judge can dismiss n>200 as underpowered.
Secondary Analysis
ANOVA + Tukey's HSD

One-way ANOVA tests whether any scaffold geometry significantly differs in pore retention %.

H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = μ₃ = μ₄ = μ₅ = μ₆ · · · α = 0.05

If significant (p<0.05): Tukey's HSD post-hoc identifies which specific pairs differ. Key comparison: Group 6 (Hybrid) vs Group 1 (Control).

Effect size: η² = SS_between / SS_total — η² > 0.14 is a large effect.

⚠️ If ANOVA Is Non-Significant
n=3 per group gives ~65-70% power for large effects. If p>0.05, report F and p honestly, shift emphasis to pore-level Spearman, and explain the power limitation. This is a predetermined contingency, not a failure.
Why Spearman instead of Pearson correlation?
Spearman is appropriate here for two reasons. First, the S scores are composite weighted ranks, not continuous measurements — they encode relative ordering, not absolute magnitudes. Spearman measures monotonic relationships, which is exactly what you're claiming: higher S score should correspond to higher pore retention, regardless of whether the relationship is perfectly linear. Second, pore retention % may not be normally distributed, and Spearman makes no distributional assumptions. If both were normal and linear, Pearson would give identical results for the ranking question.

Category: Algorithm Validity
Questions About the Scoring Engine
Why are your scoring weights justified?
The weights are derived from tissue engineering literature: pore retention is the dominant scaffold performance metric (30% weight). Boundary fit and strain compatibility each get 20% because they capture the two key spatial matching properties. I also ran a weight sensitivity analysis varying each weight ±0.10 — the ranking was stable in 7/11 perturbations. Critically, the pore-level Spearman correlation provides empirical validation: if the scoring function captures the right signal, the correlation will be significant regardless of exact weights.
Your model is a simplified elastic plate approximation. How can you trust results from a model that ignores viscoelasticity, nonlinearity, and material heterogeneity?
The Airy stress function provides a physically grounded first-order approximation of strain distribution that is computationally tractable at this research level. The simplification is acknowledged in Section 13.2. What matters for scaffold design is the relative stress distribution — which regions are high-stress vs low-stress — not the absolute magnitudes. A linear elastic model is sufficient to distinguish boundary-concentrated stress from interior-diffuse stress, which is all the scoring engine requires. Full FEA is explicitly listed as future work. This is a scientifically honest scope limitation, not a methodological error.
How do you know your wound phantom is representative of real wounds?
The phantom was deliberately designed with three geometric features — a narrow region, a high-curvature region, and a wide irregular region — to stress-test all three zone types the hybrid scaffold generator can distinguish. A circular or rectangular wound would not exercise the boundary feature computation meaningfully. The goal isn't to represent the average wound; it's to demonstrate that the algorithm can handle geometric complexity. In clinical deployment, any wound boundary image would feed into the same pipeline.
Category: Mathematics
Deep Math Questions
What does ∇⁴φ = 0 physically mean?
It means the Airy stress function cannot curve too aggressively in any direction. ∇² is the Laplacian — it measures how a function curves in space. ∇⁴ applies this twice. Setting it to zero enforces that any curvature in φ must be balanced by equal and opposite curvature somewhere nearby. This constraint encodes the laws of elastic equilibrium — that internal forces within the material must sum to zero everywhere in the absence of body forces. It's the mathematical expression of the material being in static equilibrium.
Why does σ_xx = d²φ/dy² (second derivative in Y, not X)?
This is counterintuitive but mathematically necessary. The Airy stress function is defined such that the stress components are second derivatives of φ in the PERPENDICULAR direction. σ_xx (horizontal stress) is the second derivative in y; σ_yy (vertical stress) is the second derivative in x. This follows from deriving the compatibility equations for a 2D elastic body and introducing the stress function to automatically satisfy equilibrium. The derivation is in Timoshenko & Goodier (1951). The sign convention for τ_xy being negative also comes from this derivation.
Why use von Mises stress rather than just maximum principal stress?
Maximum principal stress (σ₁) only captures the largest single stress component. Von Mises stress captures the combined distortional energy from all stress components, including shear. For a scaffold pore that can be loaded from any direction, von Mises is the appropriate scalar because it measures the total mechanical demand regardless of loading direction. Research on scaffold architecture (Hendrikson et al. 2017, Frontiers in Bioengineering) confirms that von Mises stress distribution is the standard metric for evaluating scaffold mechanical performance.
Category: Novelty & Significance
Questions About What's New
Topology optimization of scaffolds already exists (Liu et al. 2025 got 39% improvement). What does your project add?
Liu et al. optimized scaffold geometry within a single material class using FEA-based topology optimization — a powerful but computationally intensive approach. My contribution is different in three ways: (1) I use the Hat monotile, an aperiodic geometry that has never been characterized in any scaffold application; (2) I incorporate wound-boundary geometry as a direct input, making the design adaptive to patient-specific wound shapes; (3) My scoring engine is validated at the pore level against physical measurements, providing ground-truth calibration of the computational predictions. These are complementary, not competing, contributions.
If you had a bigger lab with more resources, what would you do differently?
Three things: First, full FEA in Ansys to replace the Airy stress function approximation — this would capture viscoelasticity and geometric nonlinearity. Second, PCL or PLGA printing instead of TPU, to match clinical scaffold materials. Third, fibroblast cell culture on the printed scaffolds to validate that pore retention under mechanical loading actually correlates with cell survival and proliferation. These are all in Section 13.3. The current project deliberately scopes to geometric validation of the algorithm — which is the math contribution — leaving material and biological validation for future work.
